After installing SiPRO module, the next step is to configure the settings that control how the module evaluates products, classifies inventory, and calculates replenishment recommendations.
 
All configuration is managed from the module’s settings screen.
 
How to access settings
 
 
1. Open Inventory Risk Mitigation.
2. Click Settings.

1. Stock Replenishment Strategy
 
 

This setting defines the replenishment logic used by the module.
 
Available Options
 
•Periodic Consolidated Stock Replenishment
•Order as required
 
Periodic Consolidated Stock Replenishment is designed for businesses that prefer planned replenishment cycles. In this mode, the module considers forecast holding, replenishment frequency, lead time demand, and safety stock when calculating the recommended maximum stock level and reorder quantity.
 
Order as required is designed for businesses that replenish only when needed. In this mode, the module uses the current maximum quantity as the order-up-to level and calculates the reorder quantity based on the gap between current stock on hand and the current maximum.
 
 
Recommendation
 
Use Periodic Consolidated Stock Replenishment if your business uses planned procurement cycles or wants the module to drive structured replenishment recommendations.
 
Use Order as required if your business typically replenishes reactively and does not operate on fixed replenishment intervals.

2. Consumption Period
 
 
Setting
 
•Relative consumption data in months
 
Allowed Range is between 6 to 12 months
 
This value determines how many months of recent demand history are used when calculating variability and smoothing-based measures that support safety stock and replenishment decisions.
 
A shorter period makes the model more sensitive to recent demand changes.
 
A longer period gives a broader historical view and may reduce the impact of short-term fluctuations.
 
 
Recommendation
 
•use 6 to 8 months if demand patterns change quickly
•use 9 to 12 months if demand is relatively stable and you want a broader trend view
 
For most businesses, 12 months is a practical starting point.

4. Defined Consumption Quantity for Inventory Classification
 
 
The module uses the product’s average monthly smoothed consumption to determine whether an item belongs in:
 
•X: high-demand item
•Y: medium-demand item
•Z: low-demand item
 
Editable Values
 
You can directly maintain:
 
•Y Higher bandwidth Qty
•Z Higher bandwidth Qty
 
The related lower and upper boundaries are calculated automatically.
 
How Classification Works
 
In practice:
 
•the highest-demand products fall into X
•medium-demand products fall into Y
•lower-demand products fall into Z
 
If a product is treated as non-stock by the module, it is assigned ZZ.
 
 
Recommendation
 
Set quantity thresholds that match the normal sales or usage pattern of your business.
 
If your business deals in very low-volume items, lower thresholds may be more appropriate.
 
If your business handles high-volume items, you may need significantly higher thresholds so the classification remains meaningful.

5. Service Level Based on Average Monthly Sales / Consumption and Unit Price
 
 
 
This section is one of the most important configuration areas in the module.

It defines the preferred service level for each combined inventory class:
 
•AX, AY, AZ
•BX, BY, BZ
•CX, CY, CZ
 
What This Means
 
Each code combines:
 
•product value classification: A, B, or C
•demand classification: X, Y, or Z
 
Example:
 
•AX = high value, high demand
•CZ = low value, low demand
 
For each combination, you select the preferred service level percentage.
 
Why It Matters
 
The selected service level drives the Z-value used in the safety stock formula.
 
This means the service level settings directly influence:
 
•safety stock
•reorder point
•recommended stock holding levels
 
Typical Configuration Logic
 
A business may choose:
 
•higher service levels for critical, fast-moving, or high-risk items
•lower service levels for slower-moving or less critical items
 
Example policy direction:
 
•AX, BX, CX: usually higher service levels
•AZ, BZ, CZ: usually lower service levels
 
 
Recommendation
 
Set service levels in line with your operational goals:
 
•if service availability is critical, use higher percentages
•if reducing stock investment is more important, use more conservative values

6. Stock Replenishment Frequency (in Weeks)
 
 
This section defines how often products should be replenished based on:
 
•inventory classification
•lead-time grouping
•freight method grouping
 
How This Section Works
 
Each classification row represents an inventory risk category:
 
•AX, AY, AZ
•BX, BY, BZ
•CX, CY, CZ
•ZZ
 
Each row contains three replenishment frequency values:
 
•Delivery Lead time 1–2 weeks
•Delivery Lead time 3–6 weeks
•Delivery Lead time 7+ weeks
 
These represent replenishment frequency in weeks for products that fall into that classification.
 
How the Module Uses It
 
For each product, the module:
 
1. calculates the product’s inventory classification
2. determines the lead-time bucket
3. selects the matching replenishment frequency from this matrix
4. uses that frequency in the reorder calculation
 
How to Configure It
 
Enter the number of weeks that best reflects how often you want to replenish products in each class.
 
Example approach:
 
•higher-priority classes such as AX may have shorter replenishment cycles
•lower-priority or slower-moving classes such as CZ may have longer replenishment cycles
•ZZ is used for non-stock items and normally remains 0
 
 
Recommendation
 
Start with simple business rules:
 
•fast-moving and important items: shorter intervals
•slower-moving and less critical items: longer intervals
•non-stock items: keep at 0

7. Suggested Configuration Sequence
 
For a first-time setup, it is recommended to configure the settings in the following order:
 
1. Set the Stock Replenishment Strategy.
2. Set the Consumption Period.
3. Configure the Value classification thresholds.
4. Configure the Quantity classification thresholds.
5. Configure the Service Level matrix.
6. Configure the Replenishment Frequency matrix.
7. Save the settings.
8. Review products in the Proposal Report.
 
This sequence helps ensure the classification logic is defined before replenishment outputs are reviewed.

8. Best Practice Recommendations
 
Start Simple
 
Begin with a practical first version of the settings rather than trying to perfect every number immediately.
 
Align With Business Policy
 
Your thresholds and service levels should reflect:
 
•product importance
•cost sensitivity
•demand predictability
•desired customer service level
•purchasing and freight cycles
 
After saving the settings, open the Proposal Report and review a sample of products from different categories to ensure the outputs align with business expectations.
 
 
Refine Over Time
 
It is normal to adjust the values after reviewing live product results.
